I  ·  Poetry SUTRAS सूत्र

Verses drawn from the space between breath and death

Sutra I  ·  सूत्र १

The Vampire's Lament

पिशाच का विलाप

I have drunk from ten thousand throats
and still thirsted — not for blood
but for the one vein that runs
beneath all skin, beneath all time:
the river Shiva rides to dissolve.

🌹
Sutra II  ·  सूत्र २

Dante at the Ganga

गंगा पर दांते

Nine circles — he mapped the descent.
But the Mahabharata knew:
every circle burns inward
until the flame is you,
and you are the flame, and the flame is free.

🌺
Sutra III  ·  सूत्र ३

Arjuna's Dark Night

अर्जुन की रात्रि

Between the armies, a garden —
jasmine threading through skulls,
roses fed by ancestors' blood.
Krishna says: the dead are not dead.
The garden says: neither are you.

💀
Sutra IV  ·  सूत्र ४

Nataraja in the Dark Club

नटराज अंधेरे में

Shiva dances where the strobes
become the fires of Varanasi.
Beneath his foot, Apasmara
— forgetting — writhes, and in that dance
the whole wretched world is held.

🌹
Sutra V  ·  सूत्र ५

Kali's Garden

काली का बगीचा

She grows roses in the eye sockets
of those who only loved the beautiful.
In her garden, everything blooms twice —
once in flesh, once in bone,
and the second blooming is the true one.

🌸
Sutra VI  ·  सूत्र ६

The Trident

त्रिशूल

Creation, preservation, dissolution —
three tines of one weapon.
The mystic knows: the wound
and the healing are the same thrust,
and Shiva is the hand that holds both.

III  ·  Prose MANTRAS मंत्र

Sacred fictions — myths that remember themselves through us

Current Chapter  ·  वर्तमान अध्याय

The Garden at the End of the Kali Yuga

कलियुग के अंत में बगीचा

A novella in four acts — where a half-demon dhampir wanders the burning age and finds, in a ruined temple overgrown with roses and jasmine, that moksha is not escape but the willingness to be consumed.

🌸 💀 🌹

The city of Varanasi had burned for three hundred years before anyone noticed. Not with fire — though fire was always present, always faithful at the ghats — but with the slow, interior combustion of an age collapsing into itself. The Kali Yuga was not ending so much as it was concentrating, like poison reducing in a copper pot, becoming purer and more terrible with every generation.

You are not in the body. The body is in you. And you are in Brahman, who is in everything — even this darkness, even this blade, even this particular darkness that wears your name.

— Chapter I, The Dhampir's First Teaching

D arrived at dawn, which in this city was indistinguishable from dusk. The roses had taken the old temple entirely: climbing every lingam, threading through the ribcage of a stone Nataraja whose arms still moved — or so the locals claimed — when Shiva was dreaming. Jasmine filled the air with something between prayer and intoxication. Skulls lined the courtyard walls, each one garlanded, each one fed daily with milk and the blood of willingly-given pomegranates.

He had come for the manuscript. Every demon-hunter heard the rumor eventually: a text older than Sanskrit, older than the Vedas, written in a script that was also a mantra — to read it was to recite it, and to recite it was to be changed absolutely. The Mahabharata called it the weapon that cannot be unloosed. Dante, who had encountered a fragment in the ninth circle, called it simply la voce che non finisce — the voice that does not end.

The trident does not choose its throw. Neither does the truth. Both arrive before you know they have been aimed.

— The Gita, rendered for the end of time

The rose at the centre of the garden was black. Not dyed, not metaphorically — genuinely, optically black, absorbing every frequency of light, including the frequencies that humans cannot perceive but that D, half-child of the night, could read like text. It was reading him back. In its depths, something was performing the tandava — the dance of destruction that is also the dance of creation, Nataraja's foot eternally lifting and eternally falling.
